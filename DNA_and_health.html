DNA AND HEALTH

Genetic variants come in many flavors: they can span one nucleotide or whole parts of chromosomes; they can manifest as the result of our environment (often known as mutations) or be inherited from our parents; they can be harmless or detrimental to our health. Unfortunately, today we are lacking unrestanding of most variations appearening in our genomes. Only about 0.03% of observed variants have been specified as benign or harmful; the rest are generally termed as "unknown", "uncertain", "likely benign", or "likely pathogenic". THE NETWORK OF NETWORKS

What is important to note is that regardless of whether the effect of the variant is pathogenic of benign, it will almost always alter the function of the containing element - a gene, for example. The impact of this becomes apparent when we learn just how interconnected all DNA elements are. As mentioned earlier, coding for proteins is only one of many functions of the DNA; many of its elements fulfil a regulatory purpose. For example, some genes regulate the function of other genes, by either turning them on or off, or altering the amount of a certain protein produced by that gene. As an oversimplified example imagine this: you have a gene responsible for making your pancreas produce insulin. Imagine too, that you have aquired a mutation in that gene which makes it dysfunctional. Lo and behold, your pancreas don't know when and/or how to produce insulin: either too little is secreted (enter diabetes), or too much (this could cause hypoglycemia). This seems simple enough - deal with the said mutation, alleviate the symptoms.

Unfortunately, there can be thousands to millions DNA elements far outside of this gene region which can cause it to malfunction. So you might find unstable insulin levels even when the insulin-coding gene is perfectly typical. And now you have millions building blocks to examine, hoping to find where the offending nucleotide sequence resides.

WHEN IT COMES TO HEALTH AND HEALTHCARE

Sure there's a lot going on inside our DNA already, but the external environment - our lifestyle, the food we eat, the air we breathe, the medications we take - also cause changes to our DNA. Therefore, our lifestyle and environment are the additonal contributors to our genetic diversity. The non-hereditary skin cancer, for example, is often the result of the DNA degradation in your skin cells from the extended UV exposure.

The medications and supplements we take as treatment or prevention measures often induce changes to our DNA. Taking into account the already existing variety in our genetic networks, it is almost impossible to predict the full extent of potential effects of the drug on the body. It is important to note that the gene-drug interaction works both ways. For example, a certain gene may inactivate a specific biological pathways involved in the health condidion targeted by a drug, thus inactivating the drug. Considering the information introduced so far, following conclusions can be drawn.



  • DNA is responsible for regulating every process in a human body.

  • Human genome is incredibly diverse. Only 0.4% variance between each person's genetic make-up is responsible for more than 7 billion drastically different phenotypes.

  • There are over 650 million genetic variants, and over 99% of those have an effect on our health that is unknown to us.

  • We are mostly unaware of the mechanics and effects of the interactions within gene networks.

  • The unique make-up of our genomes does not only specify our health profile, but interacts robustly with medical interventions.
